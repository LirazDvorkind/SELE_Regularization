# Generic SELE Reconstruction — Goals and Guiding Principles

This document captures the *why* behind the current direction of work. It is not a plan or a spec — it's the context an AI assistant (or a future you) should read before touching the regularization code, so that decisions stay aligned with the longer-term goal instead of optimizing for the current GaAs test case.

---

## 1. What we are actually trying to build

The original paper (Yeshurun et al.) reconstructs SELE for **a single material** (p-type GaAs wafer) using **Tikhonov regularization with a second-derivative operator**, sweeps κ over a wide range, takes the L-curve knee, and averages solutions in a confidence window. This works well for that material because:

- The expected SELE shape is smooth almost everywhere.
- The second-derivative penalty matches the physical prior ("S(z) is twice differentiable, with curvature of order 1/L_diff²").
- The κ-sweep + averaging gives a free uncertainty band.

Our actual goal is **broader**: a reconstruction tool that works on SELE curves of *arbitrary* materials, including multi-layer stacks (e.g. perovskite/Si tandems, GaAs/AlGaAs heterostructures, thin films on absorbing substrates), where:

- The SELE shape may have **interfaces** (sharp jumps or kinks where one layer meets another).
- We do **not** know the material's diffusion length, surface recombination velocity, or bulk lifetime in advance — we cannot generate a high-fidelity training set the way we did for GaAs.
- The only inputs we are guaranteed to have are the **photogeneration matrix G** (computable from the layer stack's optical constants and geometry) and the **measured ELE vector B**.
- The user should be able to point the tool at a new material and get a reasonable SELE without re-tuning any hyperparameters by hand.

That last point is the hardest part. It rules out approaches that require per-material training data, per-material κ tuning, or per-material weight schedules.

## 2. What's wrong with what we're doing now

Our current `MODEL_SCORE_GRAD` solver has accumulated several problems that, taken together, make it the wrong direction for the broader goal — even though each individual piece is defensible in isolation.

### 2a. We are validating circularly
We trained the score model on synthetic SELE curves generated by an analytical formula (`calc_Sp2` with parameters D, S, τ swept in known ranges), then tested it on curves drawn from the **same generator**. Whatever the score net learns is "the prior is shaped like the generator." We have no evidence the method generalizes to a SELE curve that doesn't come out of that formula — and the entire point of the broader goal is to handle curves that don't.

### 2b. Hand-tuned hyperparameters don't transfer
Even within the GaAs family we keep retuning REG_WEIGHT, LR_MAX, MOMENTUM, T0 every time the training data or G matrix changes. A new material would mean starting that whole loop over. A generic tool cannot work this way; it has to choose its own regularization weight from the data.

### 2c. The score-prior + NAG combination is fragile
The interaction between data gradient magnitude, score magnitude, normalization, preconditioning, and learning rate has many failure modes (per-element weighting kills the score's magnitude; full Hessian preconditioning shrinks the data gradient; momentum overshoots; LR schedule ends in the wrong basin). Each fix exposes the next one. This kind of brittleness is a sign that the *formulation* is wrong, not the *constants*.

### 2d. We have no measurement noise model
The data fidelity term is just `||GS - B||²`, which implicitly assumes Gaussian noise of unit variance on every wavelength. Real measurements have a per-wavelength uncertainty (detector noise, calibration error, photon counting statistics) that should weight the residual, and that uncertainty is what tells us "how hard to push data fidelity" — i.e. it's what should set the regularization weight automatically.

### 2e. We dropped uncertainty quantification
The paper's κ-sweep + averaging produces a mean solution **and** an uncertainty band, which is essential for a measurement tool — a SELE estimate without error bars is not actionable. Our score-grad solver produces a single point estimate and gives you nothing about how much to trust it.

### 2f. We are conflating "smooth" and "physical"
Our score model encodes "looks like a curve from the GaAs generator." It doesn't encode "satisfies the diffusion equation in this layer" or "has the right curvature given this absorption coefficient." A curve can be both smooth and physically wrong, and a curve can be physically right but not smooth (e.g. interface at an internal layer boundary). We need a prior that respects the physics, not just the visual smoothness of one material.

## 3. Principles the new approach should respect

These are the constraints any candidate method should satisfy. They come directly from the broader goal in §1 plus the lessons in §2.

1. **Material-agnostic.** The method must take only `(G, B, optional_noise_model)` as input. No per-material training, no per-material hyperparameter tuning.
2. **Self-tuning.** The regularization weight (and any auxiliary parameter) must be picked automatically from the data, ideally via the **discrepancy principle** — pick the weight where the residual `||GS - B||` matches the known noise level. This is the standard self-tuning approach in inverse problems and is theoretically grounded.
3. **Edge-aware.** The prior should not over-smooth interfaces. A second-derivative penalty actively penalizes the kind of features we expect in multi-layer stacks. We need either a Total Variation penalty (which preserves jumps), or a learned prior that has seen interfaces during training, or both.
4. **Uncertainty-aware.** The output should be a SELE estimate **with** an uncertainty band, not a single curve. This rules out purely deterministic point-estimate methods. Acceptable approaches: bootstrapping over noise realizations, sampling from a posterior (DPS-style), or ensemble methods.
5. **Convergent and predictable.** Any iterative solver should have a convergence guarantee (or at least monotone descent on a known objective). This rules out the current ad-hoc "score gradient + data gradient + adaptive weight + cosine LR" stack, which has no objective function it is provably minimizing.
6. **Cheap to deploy.** A user pointing the tool at a new material should not have to wait for training. Either the method does no training (TV) or training happens once on a universal dataset that covers many materials (universal dictionary).
7. **Honest about validation.** We should test on curves the method has *not* seen the generator of. That means testing TV on curves from `calc_Sp2`, and testing any learned method on curves from a *different* generator (e.g. a finite-element simulation, or a published SELE from a different paper).

## 4. How to read the two direction files

Two candidate directions live alongside this file:

- **`TV_DIRECTION.md`** — Total Variation regularization with discrepancy-principle auto-κ. Classical, well-understood, edge-preserving, no training needed. The honest baseline that any learned method has to beat.
- **`ML_DIRECTION.md`** — Universal-dictionary learned prior + Plug-and-Play ADMM. More work, more capability, only justified if TV is genuinely insufficient.

The recommended order is **TV first**. Not because TV is necessarily the final answer, but because:

- It is the principled baseline. If we don't measure how good TV is, we will keep building learned methods that we can't compare to anything.
- It already exists in the codebase (`tikhonov_total_variation.py` is implemented for the single-material case).
- It will surface the *real* hard cases — the curves where smoothness-with-edges genuinely isn't enough — and those are exactly the cases we'd want to design a learned prior around.

Only after we have a working TV+discrepancy baseline and know where it fails should we invest in the ML direction. That ordering protects us from the trap we are in now: spending months on a learned method whose failures we can't diagnose because we have nothing to compare to.

---

## 5. What this means for the existing score-grad code

`src/regularization/score_model/score_model_grad.py` should be treated as **a research artifact, not the path forward**. It's worth keeping for reference (it captures real lessons about preconditioning, score-vs-gradient scaling, normalization), but new work should not extend it. New work should go into a fresh solver that implements either TV+discrepancy or a Plug-and-Play loop with a properly trained universal prior.

The d32/d500 checkpoints similarly should be treated as artifacts of one specific GaAs experiment. They are not generic SELE priors and should not be marketed (to ourselves or anyone else) as such.
